What is FloatJet? 

Originally called Floop!, FLoatJet is a series of small, lightly coded feedback generation activities aimed at growing and progressing the literacy of users to feed back on their involvement in community projects at their own pace. 

Although it can be easy to evidence need for community projects, developing feedback from partipants can often be much more difficult and lead to bias that favours the "most responsive" service users, creating a situation where instead of feedback being holistic, experiences are ground into a perfect portrait or singular experience which expresses positivity instead of reality (which could be positive!).

A major problem with existing feedback loops for community activities is that they are based on gathering a range of socio-emotional markers of personal and development growth including features like self-confidence, self-efficacy, self-awareness, etcetera. Although there are definitely individuals who are willing to provide answers to these questions, the average literacy rate among the entire UK population is equivalent to that of an 8-year-old girl. Why are we forcing critical thinking on an eight-year-old girl? 

FloatJet is an app that encourages service users to use any or all of four colour-blind accessible colours to draw a picture. When the picture is submitted, it is saved locally to a gallery page. The application can be easily replicated to be saved into local computer memory, meaning it can be run without the need for an internet connection.This harm reduction strategy increases the opportunity of face to face engagement and supports the use of the tool with populations who will contribute to the gallery. The gallery can then be saved as a PDF with a dated save name for forwarding, retrieval and review. 

How to use pictures as evidence of participation:

Pictures tell a thousand words! 

1. The Use of Space and Placement

How a child occupies the page can signal their level of comfort or dominance within the activity.

    Centrality: Figures or objects placed in the center often represent the most significant part of the activity for the child.

    Size: Larger figures usually indicate importance or a sense of power. If the child draws themselves very small compared to the equipment or other participants, they may feel overwhelmed by the task.

    Grounding: Are the figures floating, or is there a "baseline" (ground)? Floating figures can sometimes suggest a lack of stability or a feeling of being "lost" in the process.

2. Line Quality and Pressure

The physical act of drawing reveals the child's energy levels and emotional state during the activity.

    Heavy Pressure: Deep, dark lines that almost indent the paper can suggest high energy, frustration, or intense focus.

    Light/Skaky Lines: Faint, broken lines might indicate hesitation, low confidence in their performance, or physical fatigue.

    Fluidity: Smooth, continuous curves often point to a sense of "flow" and ease with the activity.

3. Inclusion and Exclusion

What the child chooses to leave out is often as important as what they put in.

    The "Omission" Rule: If a child participated in a soccer game but draws the field without any other children, they may have felt isolated or focused purely on the mechanical task rather than the social aspect.

    Detailing: High levels of detail (buttons on a shirt, laces on shoes) usually correlate with high engagement and "presence" during the event.

    Exaggeration: If a child draws themselves with giant hands while playing a piano, it shows they are hyper-aware of the specific body parts required for that participation.

4. Color Psychology (Contextual)

While it’s a myth that "red always means anger," color choices do reflect mood.

    Warm Colors (Red, Orange, Yellow): Often linked to high-engagement, excitement, or extroverted participation.

    Cool Colors (Blue, Green): Can suggest a calm, focused, and methodical approach to the task.

    Monochrome: Using only one color despite having others available might suggest a desire to finish the task quickly or a lack of emotional investment in that specific moment.

5. The "Narrative" Approach

The most effective way to interpret a drawing is to ask the child to tell the story of the picture. > Pro Tip: Instead of saying "What is that?", try "Tell me about this part of the drawing." This prevents the child from feeling they "failed" to draw something recognizably, and allows them to explain their participation in their own words.
Feature	Possible Interpretation
Missing Limbs	Feeling ineffective or unable to complete the task.
Overlapping Figures	Complex social dynamics or feeling "crowded" by the activity.
Emphasis on Tools	A focus on the technical/mechanical side of participation rather than the social side.

Dudley MakerSpace

Dudley Makerspace was a community demonstration project I co-founded in 2017 with the intention of creating a dual-aspect community arts gym and outpatient non-prescribing mental health rehabilitation support service. The Makerspace ran for 21 months before it reached an end-point and was closed. 

This webpage gathers information about the Makerspace, including its business plan and a grant application so that others may review and reimagine the idea.
